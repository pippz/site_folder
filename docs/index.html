Blue Marble history:

The Blue Marble is one of the most reproduced photographs in history.
It was taken on December 7, 1972 by the crew of Apollo 17 — the last crewed Moon mission — at a distance of about 45,000 km from Earth.
It was one of the first photos to show Earth fully illuminated, since the Sun was behind the astronauts as they took it.

Solar System

The Solar System is our cosmic neighborhood, centered around the Sun and filled with a diverse collection of worlds. From rocky planets to massive gas giants, icy bodies, and drifting comets, each object follows its own path while remaining bound by gravity. Formed billions of years ago, this system continues to evolve and reveal new mysteries. Exploring it not only helps us understand how planets form and move, but also gives us a deeper perspective on Earth’s place in the universe.


Age: ~4.6 billion years
Central star: The Sun

Location: Milky Way (Orion Arm)
Diameter: ~287.46 billion km (including the Kuiper Belt)
Solar System orbital speed around galaxy: ~828,000 km/h
Number of planets: 8
known moons: 200+


The Sun



The Sun is a G-type main-sequence star (G2V) located at the center of the Solar System. Composed primarily of hydrogen and helium, it generates energy through nuclear fusion in its core, where hydrogen nuclei combine to form helium, releasing vast amounts of energy. This energy radiates outward as light and heat, sustaining life on Earth and driving the dynamics of the Solar System. The Sun's immense gravitational force, containing about 99.8% of the system's total mass, governs the motion of all orbiting bodies.

Type: G-type main-sequence star

Total life time estimated: ~10.0 billion years

Core temperature: ~15 million °C
Surface temperature: ~5,500 °C
Energy output (luminosity): ~3.8 * 10²⁶ W
Hydrogen: ~74%
Helium: ~24%

Mercury is the first planet from the Sun and the smallest in the Solar System. It is a rocky planet with a trace atmosphere and a surface gravity slightly higher than that of Mars. The surface of Mercury is similar to Earth's Moon, being cratered, with an expansive rupes system generated from thrust faults, and bright ray systems, formed by ejecta.
Fun fact: On average, Mercury is the closest planet to every other planet in the solar system.





Venus



Venus is the second planet from the Sun. Similar in size and mass to Earth, Venus has no liquid water, and its atmosphere is far thicker and denser than that of any other rocky body in the Solar System. The orbits of Venus and Earth make the two planets approach each other in synodic periods of 1.6 years.

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only astronomical object known to harbor life. This is made possible by Earth being an ocean world, the only one in the Solar System sustaining liquid surface water. Earth has a dynamic atmosphere, which sustains Earth's surface conditions and protects it from most meteoroids and UV-light at entry.





Mars

Being visible with the naked eye in Earth's sky as a red wandering star, Mars has been observed throughout history, acquiring diverse associations in different cultures. Main asteroid belt


The asteroid belt is a torus-shaped region in the Solar System, centered on the Sun and roughly spanning the space between the orbits of the planets Jupiter and Mars. It contains a great many solid, irregularly shaped bodies called asteroids or minor planets.
Ceres, the only object in the asteroid belt large enough to be a dwarf planet, is about 950 km in diameter, whereas Vesta, Pallas, and Hygiea have mean diameters less than 600 km.
On average, asteroids in the main asteroid belt are separated by about 1 million kilometers, that's why the odds of a probe or a spacecraft running into one of them is incredibely low, less then 1 in a billion.

Jupiter is the fifth planet from the Sun, and the largest in the Solar System. It is a gas giant with a mass nearly 2.5 times that of all the other planets in the Solar System combined and slightly less than one-thousandth the mass of the Sun. Its diameter is 11 times that of Earth and a tenth that of the Sun. The Great Red Spot: This storm is a massive, high-pressure, red-colored anticyclone larger than Earth, active for at least 350 years. As of 2025, Jupiter has 95 officially recognized moons, the largest of them were discovered by Galileo in 1610.





Saturn



Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun and the second largest in the Solar System, after Jupiter. It is a gas giant, with an average radius of about 9 times that of Earth. It has an eighth of the average density of Earth, but is over 95 times more massive. Even though Saturn is almost as big as Jupiter, Saturn has less than a third of its mass. Saturn is the most distant of the five planets easily visible to the naked eye from Earth, the other four being Mercury, Venus, Mars, and Jupiter.

Neptune



Neptune is the eighth and farthest known planet orbiting the Sun. It is the fourth-largest planet in the Solar System by diameter, the third-most-massive planet, and the densest giant planet. Neptune is not visible to the unaided eye and is the only planet in the Solar System that was not initially observed by direct empirical observation.

Moons of the Solar System



The Solar System has many different moons, from Earth's one Moon to the dozens that orbit large planets like Jupiter and Saturn. These moons come in many forms: some are rocky, some are icy, and some have both. A few are very active, like Io with its volcanoes, while others, like Enceladus, may have liquid water hidden under thick ice. By studying these moons and their features, such as craters and plains, scientists can learn how planets and their moons formed and whether life could exist beyond Earth.

The Moon · Earth
Earth's only natural satellite. Diameter: 3,474 km. Likely formed from debris after a Mars-sized body collided with early Earth.

Europa · Jupiter
Has a vast liquid water ocean beneath its icy surface, making it one of the most promising candidates for extraterrestrial life.

Enceladus · Saturn
Shoots geysers of water vapor from its south pole through "tiger stripe" fractures, suggesting a liquid ocean beneath the ice.

Ganymede · Jupiter
The largest moon in the Solar System, even bigger than Mercury. Has its own magnetic field and signs of tectonic activity.

Titan · Saturn
The only moon with a dense atmosphere. Has rivers, lakes and seas of liquid methane and ethane on its surface.

Io · Jupiter
The most volcanically active body in the Solar System, covered in hundreds of volcanoes driven by tidal heating from Jupiter.

Triton · Neptune
Orbits Neptune backwards, suggesting it was captured from the Kuiper Belt. Has geysers of nitrogen gas and may be a twin of Pluto.

Kuiper asteroid belt


The Kuiper belt is a circumstellar disc in the outer Solar System, extending from the orbit of Neptune at 30 AU to approximately 50 AU from the Sun. It is similar to the asteroid belt, but is far larger—20 times as wide and 20-200 times as massive.
Like the asteroid belt, it consists mainly of small bodies or remnants from when the Solar System formed. While many asteroids are composed primarily of rock and metal, most Kuiper belt objects are composed largely of frozen volatiles, such as methane, ammonia, and water.
The Kuiper belt is home to most of the objects that astronomers generally accept as dwarf planets such as Pluto and Eris. The distance between obejects in the Kuiper belt is estimated to be incredibely large, possibly even greater then the main asteroid belt.














Planet 9


Planet Nine is a hypothetical ninth planet in the outer region of the Solar System.
Its gravitational effects could explain the peculiar clustering of orbits for a group of extreme trans-Neptunian objects (ETNOs): bodies beyond Neptune that orbit the Sun at distances averaging more than 250 times that of the Earth, over 250 AU.
Based on earlier considerations, this hypothetical super-Earth—mini-Neptune sized planet would have had a predicted mass of five to ten times that of the Earth, and an elongated orbit 400-800 AU.


The idea of Planet Nine developed from efforts to explain unusual clustering in the orbits of distant trans-Neptunian objects.
In 2016, Konstantin Batygin and Michael E. Brown showed this pattern was unlikely to be random, suggesting an unseen planet shaping their motion.
Since then, researchers have tested the idea using simulations and observational data from missions like AKARI and telescopes such as Subaru Telescope, while newer facilities like the Vera C. Rubin Observatory have been tasked with systematically searching for it.
The hypothesis remains actively debated as new data continues to refine or challenge the theory.


Exo Planets


Exoplanets are planets that orbit stars beyond our solar system, and over the past few decades, astronomers have discovered thousands of them using advanced telescopes and detection methods.
These distant worlds come in an incredible variety—some are gas giants larger than Jupiter, while others are rocky planets similar to Earth, and a few may even exist in the “habitable zone,” where conditions could allow liquid water to form.
Studying exoplanets helps scientists understand how planetary systems form and evolve, and it brings us closer to answering one of humanity's biggest questions: whether life exists elsewhere in the universe.
As of March 2026, NASA has confirmed the discovery of over 6,000 exoplanets.

James Webb Space Telescope Gallery


The James Webb Space Telescope is the most powerful space observatory ever built, designed to explore the universe in infrared light and reveal objects too distant, faint, or ancient for previous telescopes to detect.
Launched in 2021, it allows scientists to look back over 13 billion years, capturing stunning images of the first galaxies, star-forming regions, and even the atmospheres of exoplanets.
Its groundbreaking technology and breathtaking imagery are transforming our understanding of how the universe—and everything within it—came to be.
