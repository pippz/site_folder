Blue Marble history:

The Blue Marble is one of the most reproduced photographs in history.
It was taken on December 7, 1972 by the crew of Apollo 17 — the last crewed Moon mission — at a distance of about 45,000 km from Earth.
It was one of the first photos to show Earth fully illuminated, since the Sun was behind the astronauts as they took it.

Solar System

The Solar System is our cosmic neighborhood, centered around the Sun and filled with a diverse collection of worlds. From rocky planets to massive gas giants, icy bodies, and drifting comets, each object follows its own path while remaining bound by gravity. Formed billions of years ago, this system continues to evolve and reveal new mysteries. Exploring it not only helps us understand how planets form and move, but also gives us a deeper perspective on Earth’s place in the universe.

Location: Milky Way (Orion Arm)
Diameter: ~287.46 billion km (including the Kuiper Belt)

Number of planets: 8
known moons: 200+


The Sun



The Sun is a G-type main-sequence star (G2V) located at the center of the Solar System. Composed primarily of hydrogen and helium, it generates energy through nuclear fusion in its core, where hydrogen nuclei combine to form helium, releasing vast amounts of energy. This energy radiates outward as light and heat, sustaining life on Earth and driving the dynamics of the Solar System. The Sun's immense gravitational force, containing about 99.8% of the system's total mass, governs the motion of all orbiting bodies.

Type: G-type main-sequence star
Age: ~4.6 billion years
Total life time estimated: ~10.0 billion years

Core temperature: ~15 million °C
Surface temperature: ~5,500 °C
Energy output (luminosity): ~3.8 * 10²⁶ W
Hydrogen: ~74%
Helium: ~24%

Mercury is the first planet from the Sun and the smallest in the Solar System. It is a rocky planet with a trace atmosphere and a surface gravity slightly higher than that of Mars. The surface of Mercury is similar to Earth's Moon, being cratered, with an expansive rupes system generated from thrust faults, and bright ray systems, formed by ejecta.
Fun fact: On average, Mercury is the closest planet to every other planet in the solar system.





Venus



Venus is the second planet from the Sun. Similar in size and mass to Earth, Venus has no liquid water, and its atmosphere is far thicker and denser than that of any other rocky body in the Solar System. The orbits of Venus and Earth make the two planets approach each other in synodic periods of 1.6 years.

Life: None

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only astronomical object known to harbor life. This is made possible by Earth being an ocean world, the only one in the Solar System sustaining liquid surface water. Earth has a dynamic atmosphere, which sustains Earth's surface conditions and protects it from most meteoroids and UV-light at entry.





Mars



Mars is the fourth planet from the Sun. It is also known as the "Red Planet", for its orange-red appearance. Being visible with the naked eye in Earth's sky as a red wandering star, Mars has been observed throughout history, acquiring diverse associations in different cultures. Fun fact: Mars has the tallest mountain in the solar system, Olympus Mons is the youngest of the large volcanoes on Mars, having formed during the Martian Hesperian Period with eruptions continuing well into the Amazonian Period, and it's about 21.9 to 26 km tall

Main asteroid belt


The asteroid belt is a torus-shaped region in the Solar System, centered on the Sun and roughly spanning the space between the orbits of the planets Jupiter and Mars. It contains a great many solid, irregularly shaped bodies called asteroids or minor planets. Ceres, the only object in the asteroid belt large enough to be a dwarf planet, is about 950 km in diameter, whereas Vesta, Pallas, and Hygiea have mean diameters less than 600 km. On average, asteroids in the main asteroid belt are separated by about 1 million kilometers, that's why the odds of a probe or a spacecraft running into one of them is incredibely low, less then 1 in a billion.







Jupiter



Type: Gas giant
Age: 4.603 billion years
Distance from the Sun: 784.27 million km or 5.20 AU
Mass: 1.89813 * 10^27 kilograms
Diameter: 139,820 km
Magnetic field: Jupiter has the largest and most powerful planetary magnetic field in our solar system, with a magnetosphere 16 to 54 times stronger than Earth's.
Surface temperature: -110 °C on average
Surface: No solid surface or defined, rocky terrain to land on, it's a gas giant
Composition: hydrogen ~76% and helium ~24%
Life: None
Atmosphere: ~90% hydrogen and ~10% helium
Moons: 95 moons and counting, biggest ones are Io, Europa, Ganymede and Callisto
Ring: faint, dusty, and tenuous system discovered in 1979 by Voyager 1

Jupiter is the fifth planet from the Sun, and the largest in the Solar System. It is a gas giant with a mass nearly 2.5 times that of all the other planets in the Solar System combined and slightly less than one-thousandth the mass of the Sun. Its diameter is 11 times that of Earth and a tenth that of the Sun. The Great Red Spot: This storm is a massive, high-pressure, red-colored anticyclone larger than Earth, active for at least 350 years. As of 2025, Jupiter has 95 officially recognized moons, the largest of them were discovered by Galileo in 1610.





Saturn



Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun and the second largest in the Solar System, after Jupiter. It is a gas giant, with an average radius of about 9 times that of Earth. It has an eighth of the average density of Earth, but is over 95 times more massive. Even though Saturn is almost as big as Jupiter, Saturn has less than a third of its mass. Saturn is the most distant of the five planets easily visible to the naked eye from Earth, the other four being Mercury, Venus, Mars, and Jupiter.

Type: Gas giant
Age: 4.503 billion years
Distance from the Sun: 1.4195 billion km or 9.5 AU
Mass: 5.683 * 10^26 kg
Diameter: 120,536 km
Magnetic field: Saturn's magnetic field has north and south poles, like those on a bar magnet, and the field rotates with the planet
Surface temperature: -178 °C
Surface: No solid, defined surface, it's a gas giant
Composition: hydrogen ~75% and helium ~25%
Life: None
Atmosphere: Hydrogen ~96% and helium ~3% with traces of methane and ammonia
Moons: Saturn has 285 confirmed moons, the most of any planet in the Solar System. Titan is its largest moon.
Ring: Saturn's rings are the most extensive and complex planetary ring system in our solar system,
spanning up to 280,000 kilometers in width but only about 10-100 meters thick.

Uranus is the seventh planet from the Sun. It is a gaseous cyan-coloured ice giant. Uranus is a unique ice giant with an extreme 98-degree axial tilt, causing it to orbit the Sun on its side, often described as rolling like a ball.





Neptune



Neptune is the eighth and farthest known planet orbiting the Sun. It is the fourth-largest planet in the Solar System by diameter, the third-most-massive planet, and the densest giant planet. Neptune is not visible to the unaided eye and is the only planet in the Solar System that was not initially observed by direct empirical observation.

Moons of the Solar System



The Solar System has many different moons, from Earth's one Moon to the dozens that orbit large planets like Jupiter and Saturn. These moons come in many forms: some are rocky, some are icy, and some have both. A few are very active, like Io with its volcanoes, while others, like Enceladus, may have liquid water hidden under thick ice. By studying these moons and their features, such as craters and plains, scientists can learn how planets and their moons formed and whether life could exist beyond Earth.

Pluto is a dwarf planet in the Kuiper belt, a ring of bodies beyond the orbit of Neptune. It is the ninth-largest and tenth-most-massive known object to directly orbit the Sun. It is the largest known trans-Neptunian object by volume by a small margin, but is less massive than Eris.
